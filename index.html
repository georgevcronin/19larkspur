Who This Covers

The five permanent housemates — George Cronin, George Bale, Alexander Pear, Freddie Austin Smith, and Oliver Shawyer — are bound by every rule here, all the time, while in the house. Visitors are signed in with a Visitor Pass and are subject to the same rules while on the premises. An EVP (Executive Visitor Pass) is an upgrade for regulars: it lets a visitor vote in committee elections, but they have to earn it first (see below).

Common Situations

Real scenarios, and what actually happens.

"The kitchen's a mess and it's not mine."

If a reasonable person would say it's been left in an unacceptable state, a 12-hour grace period starts. Still bad after that? A Kitchen Committee member — or any housemate — can start a prosecution. Art. 11

"Someone's being too loud in the living room."

Judged case-by-case against the usual standard for that time and situation. A 6-hour grace period runs on time actually spent in the house — it pauses when they leave and resumes when they're back. Art. 12

"I need to use the other floor's bathroom."

Get a Temporary Bathroom Pass countersigned by an owner of that floor — max 3 times a week. An owner can refuse with a valid reason (reasonable-person test; disputed refusals go to tribunal). No pass is no excuse — "I forgot" doesn't work as a defence. Art. 13

"Someone's bedroom smells and it's leaking into the corridor."

That's a cross-jurisdiction offence between the Bedroom and Corridor Committees — it automatically triggers the Inter-Room Committee. Art. 14, 10

"It's after 11pm and someone won't keep it down."

Curfew runs 11pm–7am. Whether a bedroom door is open or closed is entirely the occupant's call — but they own responsibility for any noise either way. Art. 14

"A friend is coming round and wants to eat with us."

Sign them in with a Visitor Pass. They get a 20-minute Temporary Cutlery Pass, and can take out a fresh one back-to-back for as long as they're here, as long as passes never overlap. Art. 2, 11

"I want to get my partner or a regular friend an EVP."

They need to: visit at least twice a week, join a house social outing weekly, and be known and accepted by every housemate for a month. The Committee Committee checks eligibility, then the housemates (not existing EVP holders) vote to ratify. Max 5 EVP holders at any time. Art. 3

"I think someone's committed an offence."

Any housemate or EVP holder can bring a prosecution. Serve them papers (must say "YOU'VE BEEN SERVED" in large writing) within 3 days of the offence, or the case is void. Can't find them? A reasonable attempt to deliver the papers still counts — the Location Committee rules if that's disputed. A jury of 1–5 people who aren't either of you decides guilt by majority vote; a tied jury is broken by a housemate's sibling with no connection to either side. Art. 18–20

"The offence spans more than one room."

The Inter-Room Committee handles it: the person prosecuting nominates which committees are involved, those committees vote on who else needs a seat, and each confirmed committee sends its own representative. Art. 10

"I've been found guilty and think it's unfair."

Say the phrase — "Stop it, I do not like this anymore!" — and your punishment is postponed up to a week (once per offence). If the verdict wasn't unanimous, you can also demand an appeal within 48 hours if you have evidence the original tribunal never saw; the new hearing happens within 72 hours, with a fresh jury. Unanimous verdicts can't be appealed, and you only get one appeal per offence. BoR I, Art. 22

"What actually happens to me if I'm found guilty?"

The Punishment Room has 7 tiers, from 5 to 20 minutes, sometimes with no phone, no light, or a controlled speaker — the jury can vote down whatever's proposed, as many times as it takes. Cleaning Punishments (6 tiers, one room up to the whole house) can stack on top of a Punishment Room tier or stand alone; the jury can vote those down too. Art. 16–17

"I don't think the Rules Committee is doing its job."

Anyone outside the Rules Committee can vote to impeach a member — but it has to be unanimous among everyone else, and it only removes one member at a time. A replacement is elected immediately. Art. 6

Punishments, Briefly

Two systems, which can stack: the Punishment Room (timed sit-downs, 5–20 minutes, escalating restrictions) and Cleaning Punishments (an actual chore, scaled from one room to the whole house). The jury always has the final say on whether a proposed punishment is too harsh. See the full Constitution for the exact tier tables.

Your Rights

Nothing in this Constitution can ever water these down — not even a future amendment.

  • I.You can postpone any punishment up to a week by saying the phrase, once per offence.
  • II.You're entitled to a timely tribunal — a prosecution can't be dragged out indefinitely.
  • III.You can't be tried twice for the same offence, aside from the one allowed appeal.
  • IV.Both sides can call witnesses — though nobody can be forced to show up.
  • V.Your bedroom is yours. No one enters without your explicit permission.
  • VI.You're presumed innocent until a jury says otherwise.

Changing a Rule

Only the Rules Committee can propose an amendment, and it only takes effect once the full house ratifies it by majority vote. The Bill of Rights above is permanently off-limits to amendment.
